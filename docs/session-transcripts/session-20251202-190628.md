**Assistant**: You're touching on something subtle there. That moment when "stopping a struggle" could be either giving up or letting go—what does that feel like in your body when you're with a client in that space?

**You**: I think the difference might be in the direction of attention. In acceptance, you're attending to what is. In resignation, maybe you're turning away from it, like you've decided it doesn't matter. But that's not quite right either.

**Assistant**: That's a compelling thread—the direction of attention. You say acceptance involves attending to what is, but then you pause on resignation...

What do you notice happening to your own attention when you sense a client has moved into resignation? Are you drawn toward them or do you feel something pulling back?

**You**: The paradox for me is that acceptance sometimes requires the kind of surrender that looks identical to resignation from the outside. How do I know the difference?

**Assistant**: You're naming something that sounds like it matters deeply in your work—that these two states can be indistinguishable from the outside.

When you're sitting with a client in that surrendered space, what are you tracking inside yourself that might give you a clue about which territory they're in?

**You**: It might be about what comes after. If I'm genuinely accepting something, there's energy available for other things. If I'm resigned, there's just... absence.

**Assistant**: Ah—so you're sensing into what becomes possible afterward. The presence or absence of available energy.

When you notice that "absence" with a client who seems resigned, what do you find yourself wanting to do? And what actually serves them in that moment?

**You**: Yes, that resonates. When I'm accepting, even if it's hard, I feel alive with it. When I'm resigned, there's numbness.

**Assistant**: That aliveness even in the midst of difficulty—what a profound marker. You can feel the difference in your own body between being alive with something hard versus numb to it.

What do you think allows that aliveness to remain present when you're accepting something difficult? What keeps the energy flowing rather than shutting down?
